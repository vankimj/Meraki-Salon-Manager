# Salon Owner Interview Script

**Purpose:** Run a 30-min conversation that surfaces real pain (not your hypothesis about pain). Based on Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test*.

**The three rules** (re-read these before every call):
1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** If you mention Plume Nexus before minute 25, you're doing it wrong.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future.** "What did you do last Tuesday" beats "would you ever..."
3. **Talk less. Listen more.** Aim for them speaking 75% of the time.

---

## Pre-call prep (10 minutes)

Before you get on the call:
- [ ] Look up their salon on Google. Note: # of employees, # of reviews, hours, services, neighborhood.
- [ ] Look up their Instagram. Note: posting frequency, content style, engagement.
- [ ] Identify 2-3 hooks (a recent review they responded to, a service that's unusual, a noticeable thing).
- [ ] Know their CURRENT platform if you can find it (sometimes their booking link reveals GG/Square/Vagaro).

---

## Opening (2 minutes)

Warm, peer-to-peer, NOT vendor:

> "Thanks for grabbing 30 minutes. Quick context: I own Meraki Nail Studio in Columbus — about 10 techs, been running it for [X] years. I'm working on something I built for my own salon and I'm trying to understand how other owners are running things. So this is a research call, not a sales call. Mind if I just ask you about your business for half an hour?"

If they ask "what are you building" — be vague:
> "Some salon software stuff. I'd rather not pitch you on it because I'm trying to learn what salons actually need first. Happy to share more at the end if you're curious."

This sets the right tone. They'll talk freely.

---

## Question bank — ask in any order, follow what they bring up

### Setup & history (5 min)
- "How long have you been open? How did you decide to start the salon?"
- "How many people work there now? Any of them with you since the start?"
- "What's your day usually look like?"

→ *Listening for:* their relationship to the business (lifestyle vs growth-oriented), team dynamics, daily rhythm.

### Current platform & tools (8 min)
- "What software do you use to run things?"
- "How long have you been on [platform]?"
- "Walk me through what tools your front desk has open during a busy Saturday."
- "What do you spend on software per month, all in?"

→ *Listening for:* tool sprawl (the more tools, the more pain). Whether they know their actual cost. Recent switches.

### Pain points — let them volunteer first
- "What's the most annoying part of running the salon?"
- "What did you do the last time a tech called in sick?"
- "Tell me about the last time something went wrong at the front desk."
- "If you had to pick one thing to make easier, what would it be?"

→ *Listening for:* SPECIFIC stories with details. If they say "marketing is hard" — push for "what happened the last time you tried to send a campaign?" Vague answers = no real pain. Stories = real pain.

### Money questions (5 min)
- "When was the last time you raised prices?"
- "What does it cost you when a client no-shows?"
- "How do you decide how much to spend on advertising?"
- "What software cost would make you flinch?"

→ *Listening for:* price sensitivity. What they consider "reasonable." How sophisticated their financial thinking is.

### Decision-making (3 min)
- "Last time you switched any business tool — what made you switch?"
- "Who else weighs in when you're deciding to try something new?"
- "What's a software tool you tried and abandoned? Why?"

→ *Listening for:* decision triggers. Their buying process. What kills deals.

### Closing (2 min, or skip)
Only if there's a natural opening AND they've told you something concrete that maps to a Plume Nexus capability:

> "This was super helpful. One thing you mentioned — [specific pain point they raised] — is actually exactly what I built. Mind if I send you a 5-minute video of how we handle that at Meraki?"

If they say yes → follow up within 24 hrs with a specific demo of THAT capability, not a full pitch.

If they don't take the bait → don't push. Send a thank-you note. They'll come back if/when the pain gets worse.

---

## Anti-patterns — questions NOT to ask

These get nice answers but no signal:

| ❌ Don't ask | Why it's bad |
|---|---|
| "Would you use a salon software with AI?" | They'll say yes to be polite. Tells you nothing. |
| "Do you think this is a good idea?" | Same. People are nice. |
| "Would you pay $99/mo for this?" | They'll either lie or anchor to the number you mentioned. |
| "What features should we build?" | Customers give you better problems, not better features. |
| "Are you happy with your current platform?" | Yes/no question, no story to follow. |
| "How would you describe your ideal salon software?" | Generic, hypothetical, no real signal. |

Replace with the specific past-tense versions in the bank above.

## Listening notes template

Take notes during the call (not on a laptop — a notebook or on your phone is less weird). Capture:

| Field | Example |
|---|---|
| Salon | Sarah's Beauty Bar, 6 techs, Polaris area |
| Current setup | GlossGenius + Mailchimp + paper tip jar |
| Monthly software cost | ~$220 estimated (didn't know exact) |
| Single biggest pain (their words) | "Front desk is always on the phone for cancellations" |
| Specific incident they shared | Tess called in sick last Saturday, lost ~$400 in re-bookings |
| Decision-maker | Owner alone, sometimes asks her business-y daughter |
| Recent attempted change | Tried Vagaro, gave up after 2 weeks (too clunky) |
| Strongest emotional moment | Eyes lit up when I described AI sick-call coverage |
| Their direct quote (verbatim) | "If software could do THAT, I would pay anything" |
| Next step | Send her a 5-min video of the conflict-resolution flow |
| Date / follow up | 2026-05-15 / set a reminder for 2026-05-22 |

The verbatim quote is gold — keep a running file of these. They become marketing copy, sales talk-tracks, and (years later) testimonials.

---

## After the call (within 2 hours)

While it's fresh, write down:
1. **One sentence:** what's their #1 pain in their words?
2. **One sentence:** does Plume Nexus solve that today, partially, or no?
3. **Yes/no:** would you bring them into the Founders' Cohort?
4. **One action:** what's the follow-up by when?

Keep these in a single doc (Google Sheet works fine). After 10 of these, you'll see patterns.

---

## When patterns emerge (after ~10 conversations)

Look at your notes. Ask:
- What pain did 7+ owners mention unprompted?
- What pain did NOBODY mention that you assumed everyone had?
- What did the highest-quality prospects (real budget, real authority) all say?
- What killed conversations or made them awkward?

Use these to rewrite the homepage, the chatbot system prompt, the demo script. The interview phase shouldn't end — it should become a quarterly habit even after you have paying customers.
